Kaggle: AI Driven Biomedical Hackathon - Classification Of Omics Experiments Using Natural Language Processing (NLP)

Introduction

The "MLRW 2022: AI Driven Biomedical Hackathon" is a 5 day data competition by IITG hosted on Kaggle. The dataset from the competition is a collection of tabular records of experiments obtained from omics repositories such as the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO). The objective is to train a machine learning model to classify these experiments into control or perturbation (non-controls). In doing so, this will help in the efforts to automate the data mining of such omics repository which will aid in the discovery of biological causes of diseases and novel drugs.

Unique Challenge

The number of labelled samples in the training dataset is only 623 out of 20,079 samples. The number of unlabeled samples is 19,456 and the number of test samples is 6,070. This presents with a unique challenge to train a good enough model with the limited number of labeled data.

Main Strategy: Domain knowledge-based Feature Engineering

As the number of labeled samples is low, this makes more complex models such as deep learning infeasible as they required large amounts of training data so as to not overfit. There are a few approaches to tackling such a problem. The first is to use semi-supervised learning methods such as pseudo labeling. In pseudo labeling, confident predictions obtained from the test set that are above a certain preset threshold are added as pseudo labeled samples for the model to be retrained. Pseudo labeling has been used in multiple winning solutions of Kaggle competitions. Another approach known as the data centric approach, focuses more on the quality of the data. This is in contrast to a model centric approach which focuses on complex models and hyperparameters tuning. As the competition is relatively short in duration (only about 5 days) and the labeled dataset being small, I decided to take the data centric approach and rely on careful data and feature selection, along with domain knowledge-based feature engineering.

Conclusion

By participating in this competition, it allows me to understand the challenges faced by omics researchers and how NLP can be used in the biomedical domain. The limited number of labelled data mimics real-life situations where the cost and time required to acquire labelled data is a challenge. By using semi-supervised training methods and putting strong focus on domain knowledge-based feature engineering, the final model was able to perform well and managed to obtain a good standing on the Kaggle leaderboard.
